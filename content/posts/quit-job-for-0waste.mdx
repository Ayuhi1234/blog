I want to write this one down honestly, including the parts that don't make for a great founder-story tweet, because I read a lot of those before quitting and most of them left out the boring, load-bearing details.

## The problem, not the pitch

I'm not going to lead with the pitch. I'm going to lead with the problem, because the problem is what kept me up at night for eight months before I ever wrote a line of code: small and mid-sized businesses that want to switch to sustainable packaging have no good way to find, compare, or trust suppliers. It's a fragmented market of good actors and greenwashers with no easy way to tell them apart, and the businesses that most want to do the right thing have the least time to do the research.

That's the whole idea. Everything else — 0waste, the marketplace, the verification system — is just the shape the solution ended up taking.

## The spreadsheet that changed my mind

For three months I ran 0waste as a spreadsheet and a WhatsApp group. No app, no code, just me manually matching businesses to suppliers I'd vetted by hand. Slow, unscalable, and exactly the right way to validate a market before building anything.

<Callout type="tip" title="If you're considering doing this yourself">
The unscalable version isn't a lesser version of the business — it's the fastest way to find out if the business should exist at all. I talked to 60 businesses before writing a single line of code. That conversation data shaped the product more than any amount of solo whiteboarding would have.
</Callout>

By month three, the spreadsheet had facilitated real transactions, real repeat customers, and one supplier who asked, unprompted, if there was "an app version of this." That was the signal I'd been waiting for without admitting I was waiting for it.

## The runway math I almost got wrong

I did the naive version of runway math first: savings divided by monthly expenses equals months of runway. The naive version doesn't account for the fact that your expenses change the moment you quit — health insurance, the tools you now pay for yourself instead of expensing, the tax structure of self-employment. My real runway was about 30% shorter than my first spreadsheet said. I'm glad I caught that before quitting, not after.

The actual number that made the decision easy wasn't runway in months — it was runway in **validated experiments**. I could afford to run roughly 8-10 more structured tests of the core hypothesis before I'd need to either raise money or go back to a job. That felt like enough. It's the number I'd tell anyone in this position to calculate, instead of the vaguer "how many months can I survive."

## The moment itself

There wasn't a dramatic scene. I sent the resignation email on a Tuesday morning, from the same desk I'd worked at for two years, with the spreadsheet open in the next tab. My manager was kind about it, which somehow made it harder, not easier. I remember being surprised that the overwhelming feeling wasn't excitement or fear — it was relief, the specific kind that comes from finally admitting a decision you'd already made weeks earlier.

## What I'd tell someone at the spreadsheet stage right now

Don't quit for the idea. Quit for the evidence. The idea is cheap and everyone has one. What made this decision defensible wasn't conviction — it was 60 conversations and three months of a business that worked badly, by hand, at a scale nobody would call impressive, but worked. That's the bar. Not certainty. Evidence.

I'll be writing about this build in public from here — the wins, the numbers, and the parts that don't work yet. This is post one.
