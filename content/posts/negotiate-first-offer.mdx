My first job offer, I accepted the exact number they gave me, on the spot, on the phone, because I was so relieved to have an offer that the thought of pushing back felt like tempting fate. I left money on the table that I'm confident I could have gotten just by asking. This is the post I wish someone had made me read first.

## The fear is doing more work than the risk

The single biggest barrier to negotiating a first offer isn't tactics — it's the belief that asking for more will make the company rescind the offer. In practice, companies that make you an offer have already decided they want you; a reasonable counter almost never changes that. I've since talked to enough hiring managers to say this with confidence: rescinding an offer over a polite negotiation is rare enough that it makes recruiting-horror-story threads, which should tell you how rare it actually is.

## The only script you need

You don't need to be clever. You need to be clear.

> "Thank you so much for the offer — I'm really excited about this. I'd like to take a couple of days to review it, and I'll follow up with any questions."

That buys you time without committing to anything. Then, when you're ready to counter:

> "I've had a chance to think it over, and I'm still very excited about the role. Based on my research and the other conversations I've had, I was hoping we could get closer to [number]. Is there flexibility here?"

<Callout type="tip" title="The part people skip">
Notice the script never says "I have another offer" unless you actually do. You don't need a competing offer to negotiate — you need market data and the willingness to ask a plain, polite question. Most companies build negotiation room into the initial number *expecting* a counter.
</Callout>

## Do the research before the call, not during it

Walking in with a specific number backed by data changes the entire conversation from "please give me more money" to "here's what the market says this role is worth." Levels.fyi, public compensation bands (increasingly required by law in many places), and conversations with people already in similar roles all beat guessing.

## Negotiate the whole package, not just the base

The number I almost accepted on the spot was the base salary line. What I didn't think to ask about: signing bonus, equity vesting schedule, start date flexibility, and remote work terms. On a first offer, base salary often has the least room to move — equity and signing bonus frequently have more, because they don't set a permanent internal benchmark the way base salary does.

## What actually happened when I finally did this

Second job, I asked. Not aggressively — I used almost exactly the script above. The recruiter came back within a day with an 8% bump to base and an extra week of signing bonus. Nobody got upset. Nobody rescinded anything. The entire feared confrontation was, in reality, a two-email exchange that took less time than writing this post.

## The actual lesson

Negotiating your first offer isn't really about the money, even though the money matters. It's about learning, early, that professional relationships can survive — and often respect — a polite, well-reasoned ask. That lesson is worth more than the percentage bump, because you'll use it again at every negotiation after this one.
