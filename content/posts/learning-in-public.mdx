"Learning in public" has become enough of a buzzword that I want to be specific about what I actually mean by it, because the vague version of the idea — post your journey online! — isn't what's made this useful for me. The specific version is.

## The difference between documenting and performing

There's a version of "building in public" that's really performing certainty for an audience — polished takeaways, confident conclusions, a narrative arc applied after the fact to make the messy process look intentional. I don't want to do that here, and I want to say so explicitly, because the temptation is constant.

What I actually mean by learning in public is closer to: **write things down while they're still uncertain, and let the uncertainty show.** A post about a book doesn't need to pretend I've fully synthesized it into a tidy framework. A post about a technical decision doesn't need to pretend I knew it was right before I tried it. The value, for me and hopefully for anyone reading, is in the actual texture of figuring something out — including the parts that were wrong.

<Callout type="tip" title="The habit that makes this sustainable">
I don't wait until I've "figured it out" to write. I write at the point of confusion, not just the point of resolution. Half the posts on this site started as notes-to-self about something I hadn't fully understood yet.
</Callout>

## Why write it down instead of just thinking it

Writing is a different cognitive act than thinking, and I don't fully understand why, but I trust the effect: ideas that feel complete in my head reveal their gaps the moment I try to write them in a sentence someone else could follow. This blog isn't primarily a way to share what I know. It's a forcing function to find out what I don't, dressed up as publishing.

The secondary effect — that other people occasionally find it useful — is real and I'm grateful for it, but it's not the mechanism. If nobody ever read a word of this, I'd still be writing it, because the writing is doing the work before anyone reads it.

## What this site is actually for

Ten categories, one running document: software engineering, AI, book summaries, productivity, life lessons, the 0waste build, career, travel, personal journal entries like this one, and whatever doesn't fit anywhere else. Not a portfolio. Not a personal brand exercise, even though it will inevitably function as one to some degree. Just an ongoing, honest record of what I'm learning, written close enough to the moment of learning it that it hasn't been sanded down into a tidier story yet.

## The actual invitation

If you're considering starting something like this: don't wait until you have expertise. The version of this site that would have waited for expertise doesn't exist yet, three years later, because expertise is retrospective and writing works best prospectively. Start at the point of confusion. That's the whole method.
