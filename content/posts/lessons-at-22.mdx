I keep a note on my phone titled "for 22-year-old me" that I add to every time I catch myself learning something that would have been useful a decade ago. This is that note, cleaned up.

## Nobody is coming to tell you the plan

The single biggest shift between school and everything after it: school hands you a syllabus. Nobody hands you one again. There is no authority figure who will tell you the correct five-year plan, and waiting for one to appear — a mentor, a boss, a book — is itself a way of staying stuck. The plan is something you write, badly, revise constantly, and mostly figure out by doing things and noticing what you don't want to do again.

## Your twenties are for collecting information, not for optimizing

I spent too much of my early twenties trying to make the "correct" choice — the right job, the right city, the right five-year trajectory — as if there were a hidden right answer I could reason my way into. There wasn't. The people I know who ended up somewhere they actually like mostly got there by trying things, paying attention to what energized them versus what drained them, and adjusting. Optimization requires information you don't have yet. Collect the information first.

<Callout type="quote" title="A reframe that helped more than any advice">
You're not behind. You're gathering data that people who "figured it out early" often never bothered to gather, and it shows later — in how well they actually know what they want versus how well they can perform having known it all along.
</Callout>

## Comparison has a denominator problem

Everyone you're comparing yourself to online is a numerator without a denominator — you see the outcome, never the years of unglamorous repetition or the quiet failures that didn't make the post. This isn't a novel observation, but knowing it intellectually and *feeling* it are different skills, and only the second one actually helps at 11pm when you're scrolling.

## Ask for the raise, the intro, the feedback

The worst outcome of asking for something is almost always "no," and "no" costs you nothing you didn't already not have. I didn't ask for a raise for three years out of a vague sense that asking was presumptuous. When I finally did, the answer was yes, on the first try, and I've thought about those three years of silent assumption more than I'd like to admit.

## Health is the one compounding asset you can't buy back later

This is the most boring item on the list and the one I'd bold if I could bold just one. Sleep, movement, and not treating your body like an infinitely resilient machine — these aren't productivity hacks, they're the foundation everything else sits on. I didn't take this seriously until a bad year forced me to, and I got lucky that the forcing function wasn't worse.

## The actual thesis

Most of this list reduces to one idea: you have more time and more permission to change course than it feels like at 22. The fog is real, but it's temporary, and it clears faster if you stop waiting for someone to hand you a map and start walking with the compass you already have.
