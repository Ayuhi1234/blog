I planned the first three days of this trip in detail and left the rest deliberately blank. That decision turned out to matter more than anything I packed.

## The itinerary that wasn't

Most of my travel before this trip had been optimized the way I optimize everything — dense schedules, a list of things to see, a plan for making the most of limited time. Somewhere around day four, sitting outside a tea house with no cell signal and no plan for day five, I realized how much of my normal travel style was just productivity anxiety wearing a backpack.

This trip had no such schedule. Three weeks, a rough direction, and villages along the way that may or may not have a place to sleep that night. It was uncomfortable for about four days and then it wasn't.

## What no signal actually does to you

I expected the lack of phone signal to be an inconvenience I'd tolerate for the views. What actually happened was stranger: somewhere around the one-week mark, I noticed my attention span had physically changed. I could sit and watch a valley for twenty minutes without the reflexive urge to document it, check something, or move on to the next thing. That's not a metaphor — it was a genuinely different cognitive mode than the one I live in by default, and I hadn't realized how rare it had become.

<Callout type="quote">
The mountain doesn't care about your itinerary, and after a few days, you stop caring about it either. That's not resignation — it's the actual point.
</Callout>

## The village that changed the trip

Around day twelve, a delayed river crossing meant staying an extra two nights in a village that wasn't on my rough plan at all. A family running the only guesthouse fed me the same meal three times, taught me enough of the local card game to lose consistently, and asked more questions about my life than most people I've known for years. None of it was on any itinerary. All of it is what I actually remember from the trip, more than any peak or vista.

## What actually changed when I got back

I wish I could report a permanent transformation. I can't. Within a week of returning, I was back to checking my phone the way I always had. But something calibrated differently underneath that: I now build deliberate slack into plans I make — trips, weekends, even work weeks — because I have direct, physical memory of what it felt like to have unstructured time and no way to fill it with a screen. That memory is a tool now. I reach for it when a week feels too packed.

## The actual advice, if you're considering something like this

Don't plan past day three. Book the flight, sort the permits and gear, and then deliberately leave the rest open. The discomfort of not knowing what tomorrow looks like is the whole mechanism — it's what forces the slowdown that a fully planned trip, however beautiful, quietly protects you from.
