I have tried, with complete sincerity, at least six productivity systems. Getting Things Done. Bullet journaling. A build-it-myself Notion database with fourteen linked tables. Every single one worked for about three weeks and then quietly died the way New Year's resolutions die — not in a dramatic collapse, just a slow fade into "I'll get back to it tomorrow."

The system I actually use now embarrasses me a little with how simple it is. But it's been running for over a year, which makes it the longest-lived system I've ever had. So: simple wins. Here's what it actually is.

## Three lists, nothing else

**Today.** No more than five items, written by hand every morning before I open my laptop. If it doesn't fit on an index card, it doesn't go on today's list.

**This week.** A loose holding pen for things I know need to happen in the next seven days but don't need to happen today. I review this once, on Sunday, and pull items into Today as the week goes on.

**Someday.** Everything else — ideas, half-formed projects, things I might want to do eventually. This list is where good intentions go to be honest with themselves. Most things that land here never leave, and that's fine. That's what it's for.

<Callout type="tip" title="The part that actually made this work">
The rule isn't "write down everything." It's "if it's not on one of these three lists, it doesn't exist yet, and that's okay." Most productivity systems fail because they try to capture everything with equal weight. This one succeeds by aggressively refusing to.
</Callout>

## Why my previous systems failed

Looking back, every system I abandoned had the same root problem: **the overhead of maintaining the system exceeded the value of the system.** GTD's weekly review took ninety minutes and I could never sustain it past a month. My fourteen-table Notion database took longer to update than most of the tasks inside it took to complete.

A system you can't sustain isn't a productivity system — it's a productivity *hobby*, and hobbies are fine, but they're not what I was trying to build.

## What I explicitly don't track

- Time. I don't time-block, and I don't log hours. It made me feel productive and measurably made me less so, because I spent attention managing the log instead of doing the work.
- Habits, in this system. Habits live in a separate, much dumber system (a wall calendar and a pen), because mixing "things I do once" with "things I do daily" made both lists worse.
- Anything that isn't actionable this week. "Learn Rust" isn't a task. "Do the first chapter of the Rust book" is.

## The honest caveat

This system works for the shape of my work — mostly self-directed, mostly asynchronous, mostly one person's responsibility at a time. It would probably fall apart managing a ten-person team's sprint. The lesson underneath the specific system is more useful than the system itself: **match the system's overhead to how much structure your actual work requires, not to how much structure feels impressive.**

Simple, sustained, and slightly boring has beaten every clever system I've tried. I don't expect that to change.
