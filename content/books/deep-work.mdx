Newport's core claim — that the capacity for sustained, distraction-free focus is a genuine competitive advantage, precisely because it's becoming rarer — has aged well. Reading it changed how I structure mornings more than almost any other productivity book I've read.

## What I actually adopted

A fixed deep work block, same time every day, phone in another room. Not glamorous, but it's the one habit from this book that survived contact with real life a year later, which is more than most productivity advice can claim.

## The honest critique

The book is stronger on diagnosis than prescription — the case for why deep work matters is airtight, the specific routines offered feel more like case studies of unusually disciplined people than a system an average reader can directly copy. I ended up building my own ritual rather than adopting his.

## Verdict

Essential for the argument alone, even if you build your own system rather than his.
